Network Media

Selecting the Appropriate Media

Using the proper media is an important part of network communications. Without the proper physical connection, either wired or wireless, communications between any two devices will not occur.

In most home and small office networks our network connections include:

  • LAN connections: End-user and intermediary devices that are connected within the local network.

  • WAN connections: The connection between our local network and our ISP’s network.

LAN Connections

End-devices can connect to the LAN using either a wired connection or using wireless transmissions. Notice in the figure that the computers and network printers are connected to the Ethernet switches using a straight-through Ethernet cable. The Ethernet switch may be embedded within the router or be a separate device.

Category 5 Ethernet cables are used when the end-device’s NIC card and the switch port can operate at a bandwidth up to 100 Mbps. For gigabit and higher bandwidths, Category 6 cables should be used. Category 6 cables also support the lesser bandwidths as well.

An Ethernet cross-over cable is used when needing to connect two Ethernet switches. Adding additional switches is useful to extend the number of devices in the LAN.

Note: Some switches have a Medium Dependent Interface (MDI or MDI-X) port which allows a straight-though cable to be used when connecting to another switch.

Devices can also be connected using wireless communications. It is important that the device’s WLAN NIC uses a WLAN standard that is compatible with the wireless access point. For example, an 802.11ac wireless access point will support Wi-Fi clients using 802.11a, b, g or n.

WAN Connections

To connect the LAN to the provider’s network the router must be connected to a DSL or cable modem. A straight-through Ethernet cable is used for the connection.

The DSL or cable modem needs to have a connection to the service provider. For DSL this is typically done using an RJ-11 connector and an ordinary 6 wire telephone cable. Cable modems are usually connected to the cable provider using a coaxial cable.

Note: Some home routers include an embedded DSL or cable modem.

Using the proper cables or wireless standard to make the connections is an essential first step in establishing network connectivity.
